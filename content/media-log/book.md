## My Heart Laid Bare, JCO (audiobook) {#my-heart-laid-bare-jco--audiobook}

-   everything i know about america i learned from joyce carol oates
-   the narrator speaks in such an announcer's voice, everything feels like it
    might be a scam (this is a compliment)
-   it does create a heavy-ass disatnce tho. hard to keep engaged
-   there's a point though where they distance fades and the novel
    sings. i remember why JCO's distanced style works. you get two views
    of the characters simultaneously, a public and a private, of which
    two thoughts
    1.  v. fitting the content of this book: con artists who themselves
        have to balance the half-form truth and the complete image
    2.  all the gothic novels are about public life --- down to the most
        recent being mostly real life historical figures --- so this
        style perfectly matches the subject. you have to engage with the
        distant and powerful, as you might with (contemporarily to the
        book) bill clinton or OJ simpson, or bill gates
-   as well, the end (2 parts away) feels like SUCH a build up. nice

## Random Acts of Extreme Violence, Jack Womack {#random-acts-of-extreme-violence-jack-womack}

-   Been a while since a book has actually sent me down a panic. Let's
    call it "the speckled band effect"
-   also worth noting, though, that its a very funny book. the extremes
    of violence, etc, alternate between satirical excess and gut
    punch. my theory now: seeing how Booz responds to things is where
    the real gut punches come from
-   noticing now how this is less a "its still so relevant now" book as
    it is a great exaggeration of the 90s style of urban terror. even
    the non-specifics of the "riots." its like taking the tv-news style
    terror of urban decay etc and treating the feared objects (the
    _pooors_) and treating them as subjects
-   it builds soooo well too.  didnt _quite_ stick the landing, but up
    til then it was near ideal

## War and Peace {#war-and-peace}

-   keeping with my "I love long novels" trend: the longest novel people
    think of from the top of their heads. so wild to me how tolstoy,
    noted anarchist, writes such mannered, european prose, especially
    given russian lits more... edgy(?) feel. it reminds me of the french
    stuff. i guess that's st petersburg, the same way an american novel
    will feel different in the "provinces" vs the northeast/other old
    colonial midwests. that said, i'm one "book" in. this could all be
    setup (though i remember anna karenina feeling the same)
-   3 books in: feel the same
-   4 books: i do get the Grossman Life and Fate vibes for sure, but it
    just hasn't drawn me in. i guess it fits why its a 'history' not a
    'novel' (per tolstoys opinions)
-   i liked the one book, the one with the gambling plot. this most
    recent one---the masons and the peasants plot---was also ok, but
    didnt hit as well as the last. its not quite a "hangout" book, like
    you might hope from something so long, but im for sure not hating it
-   such a long fox hunting scene, my gott!
-   overall, just a real up and down :/. The melodrama, i love. The
    social hierarchy stuff, well ok. but sometimes its just intolerably
    european and old
-   book 9, so far, i have liked. the mix of philosophy in it is great,
    and beyond the first monolog on war, real well integrated into the
    characters. its the classic "things heat up at the halfway", spread
    over something so large
-   book 9 is great. i have to say it. its where it hits its stride
-   book 10 has not matched 9 yet.... back to the slow parts. we'll see if that
    builds once it becomes a real war
-   2021-09-09: lol was that wrong. the wars are if anything more boring:
    historical reports rather than dramatic events
