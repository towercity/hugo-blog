## Fallout: New Vegas (+DLC) (started 2020) {#fallout-new-vegas--plus-dlc----started-2020}

-   If anything, this makes it clear why i avoided videogames so much in
    this era: it ain't _bad_ by any measure, but it's just this flattish
    power fantasy of mundane Apocalypse. The world-building is good, but
    nothing too interesting happens in it.
-   by the end, i was finishing it to finish it, nothing more. it might
    have been good if it was half the length, but since it was so
    long.... it felt like a chores simulator, tbh
-   a though i had while playing: a good game remains fun even when it
    gets difficult. in fact, it gets more fun the harder it
    becomes. fallout is not like this. the difficult dlcs are just a slog.


## DQXIS (Act II -  end) {#dqxis--act-ii-end}

-   the short stories before act ii are amazing in thier thematic cohesion:
    Kamyu gives up his cherished memories to continue his quest
-   also, how they add despair by leaving open whether the hero lives. they
    show a world in disrepair
-   Rou's section makes it super clear to me how much of the strength of
    Dragon Quest as a written story is how, despite its simplicity, it
    creates conflicting emotions. Rou's sadness when Randall trips and
    drops his cake is conflicted because we know it's a comparably happy
    moment to reality, in which Randall's likely dead, but all the
    sadness of the truth flows out in that moment. Randall _is_ likely
    dead, and the fantasy of happiness can't stay cohesive. If it's not
    universally, simply, and unrealistically joyous at every moment, the
    worse truth will inevitably shine through.
-   i love how freeform/wandery it gets once you get both Rou and Sylvia
    in act II. I went around hunting for quests and got caught up in the
    Galapagos and Hotto storylines by mistake. it's like a perfect mix
    of linearity and freeform
-   the dragon boss in hotto, too (perhaps underlevled), was the first
    really genuinely fun boss fight, cause i had to strategize ahead
    during the battle, and give each party member ad-hoc roles
-   i like how many times it asks you the 'do you wanna do this for
    sure' before the timeskip, and makes EVERY party member against
    it. really makes it feel like you're doing it wrong, even if its
    _technically_ the only way forward
-   that said, act III is lovely. it turns into a "mess around" sort of
    game, which the DQ games have always excelled at. you can go a lot
    of places, but w/o the right level/stats you won't get far. XI goes
    further than normal though, adding all kinds of bonus challenges and
    even full storylines. i guess this is what open world games are?
-   i beat the final boss on my first try! first time ever in a dq
    game. took nearly an hour, and i almost party wiped at least onc,
    but god was it exhilerating! only Sekiro, that i can think of, has
    so perfect of a final boss: instead of 'just a toughie', it feels
    like a culmination: don't quite know why, but it's true
-   (relection later): one great thing about DQ games is, despite their
    story-ness, they keep the gameplay the focus. the interactivity
    comes first

## SMT III {#smt-iii}

-   love it from the start: the look, the atmosphere, the dungeons. it's
    made for me
-   great example of something that gets better as it goes: once its in
    the real dungeon crawling: the puzzle and shut: despite a few
    hiccups, its the perfect 3d version of the old first-person style
-   even better: took a long break from this one, and it was easy as
    hell to step back into. one of the real advantages of this games
    style is that you're constantly putting together ad-hoc strategies
    with your demons, meaning if you forget your strategy from months
    before, so long as you remember the basic principles, its easy to
    step back to. its great for a difficult game of jrpg length
-   first real hard boss in a while: trumpeter. he can insta-kill whoevers got the
    lowest percentage hp of your team. the best thing about it is he's hard
    because he forces you to re-evaluate your strategy. it's hard the way the rest
    of the game is, not by ramping up some new, arbitrary thing
-   dying to one boss all the time though: hecka frustrating, ne. It's not easy to
    grind, or make huge team changes, so what do you do about an unblockable
    attack that hits harder than the MC can take? that said, its not a dead end so
    much as a hard puzzle that, unfortunately, requires a lot of tedium
